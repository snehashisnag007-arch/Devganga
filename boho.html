Nature reclaims the interior. Rattan, macramé, living walls, and the perpetual calm of filtered green light. A café that breathes — deeply aligned with Rishikesh's spiritual heartbeat.

02
The concept

A garden that learned to make coffee.

The Grove is the most naturally aligned concept for Rishikesh. The city is a hub for yoga retreats, wellness tourism, and the globally mobile spiritual seeker — a specific demographic that responds viscerally to organic materials, abundant plant life, and spaces that feel undesigned.

The governing principle is abundance — not one plant but dozens, not one texture but five. The interior should feel like stepping into a cultivated wilderness. Every surface that can hold a plant, does.

Furniture & seating

Woven, caned, and grown — not manufactured.

Rattan seating

Rattan egg/bucket chairs with loose linen cushions in natural white or sage. 1–2 hanging egg chairs suspended from ceiling beam. Source: Assam/West Bengal cane furniture cooperatives. Budget: ₹4,500–12,000/chair. Seat height 42cm with angled back.

Floor seating zone

Low teak platform base (15cm height, 3×3m) covered in dhurrie rug. Floor cushions 60×60cm in block-print cotton — earthy greens, terracotta, orange. Low live-edge wood tables at 30cm height on hairpin legs. Shoes-off zone.

Cane café chairs

Cane-backed chairs in natural finish with simple pad cushions for the main dining zone. Light and stackable — ideal for varying the layout. Bamboo frame bistro tables in 60cm round. Source: Moradabad craft clusters.

Macramé hammock

A macramé swing/hammock in one corner — non-functional but deeply Instagrammable and on-brand. A large jute-woven pouf cluster near the window. A handloom repurposed as a decorative room divider between zones.

Live-edge tables

Mango wood or teak live-edge slab tables on raw hairpin or weld-iron legs. No two identical — the variation is the point. 70cm round for 2-tops, larger irregular slabs for 4-tops. Source: Jaipur or Saharanpur wood markets.

Textiles

Cushion fabric: cotton block-print in ajrakh or bagru earthy tones. Curtains: sheer undyed linen. Table runners: hand-woven cotton in sage/natural. Throws on chairs: knitted cotton in warm whites. Everything hand-made, imperfect.

Plants & greenery strategy

The living décor — a full plant design.

Vertical — wall plants

Living moss wall behind the bar (2×2m min). Vertical pocket planters on south-facing wall. Tillandsia air plants on driftwood boards — zero soil, minimal care. The moss wall is the single highest-impact investment.

Hanging — ceiling canopy

Pothos and trailing ivy from ceiling hooks at varying heights. Glass globe terrariums hanging near windows. Macramé hangers with spider plants and string of pearls. 8–12 hanging plants creates the "forest canopy" effect.

Floor — statement plants

Large monstera deliciosa (1.5m+) in terracotta pots at zone transitions. Banana plant or bird of paradise near entrance. Fiddle-leaf fig by the window. Snake plants in low-light zones near bathrooms.

Table — daily ritual

Small terracotta pot on every table — succulents or herbs (mint, tulsi). Numbered terracotta pots double as table numbers. Mint and basil are functional for drinks. Dried pampas grass in tall vases at counter ends.

Maintenance plan

Local mali (gardener) 2×/week. 70% low-maintenance varieties (pothos, snake plant, spider plant). Self-watering pots for ceiling hangers. Group plants for easy watering circuits. A plant care log at the bar — it becomes a talking point.

Mirror + plant synergy

Circular mirrors (50–80cm) in driftwood or wicker frames placed directly beside plant clusters. The green reflection doubles perceived greenery at zero cost. One large arch mirror near the floor seating zone creates an illusion of a garden courtyard beyond.

"In Rishikesh, the sacred and the natural are the same thing. This café simply makes that explicit."
Façade, entrance & floor plan

Entering should feel like crossing into a garden.

Exterior façade

Warm lime-plaster finish in natural white or cream. Bamboo pergola frame over the entrance with trailing plants. Driftwood-burned name sign. Terracotta and brass bell cluster at the door. Stone pathway lined with low plants. Two large potted banana plants flanking the entrance.

Sensory entrance design

Water feature sound (small tabletop fountain just inside door). Scent: vetiver or sandalwood diffuser. Sound: soft instrumental Raga Bhimpalasi or Gayatri mantra covers at 50dB. Optional shoes-off wooden tray at entrance. The transition should feel like stepping out of the city and into a forest clearing.

Ceiling treatment

Expose raw ceiling and paint white. Install horizontal bamboo poles at 2m height — the structural "indoor canopy layer." All plant hangers and macramé suspend from these poles. Fairy lights woven through the bamboo create evening warmth. This one intervention transforms the entire spatial character.

Spiritual Rishikesh touches

Singing bowls as décor (not for use). Rudraksha bead curtain in the zone partition. Small brass Ganesha or Buddha silhouette as tasteful wall art. Hand-woven mandala tapestry on accent wall. Sanskrit quote in linen wall-hanging. Incense or diffuser with vetiver.

Investment roadmap

The plant wall first. Everything follows from there.

  • Living moss wall / vertical garden (2×2m) Essential — the room's identity
  • Large floor plants (monstera, banana, fiddle-leaf) Essential — zone anchors
  • Rattan egg chairs + 1 hanging chair Essential — signature seating
  • Floor seating platform + dhurrie rug High — most unique zone
  • Bamboo ceiling pole canopy + fairy lights High — transforms the ceiling
  • Live-edge wood tables (4–6) Medium — dining surfaces
  • Circular driftwood-frame mirrors (3–4) Medium — plant mirror synergy
  • Cane bistro chairs for main zone Layer in — affordable
