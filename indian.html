03
The concept

The Ganga is not one colour. It is all of them, in sequence.

The River is the most site-specific of the four concepts. The Ganga river — which runs through Rishikesh — flows in this exact analogous palette: deep indigo at the deepest currents, teal at the mid-depth, mint where it shallows, and near-white mist where it catches the Himalayan light. This café makes that palette explicit.

It draws on the rich craft vocabulary of North India — Rajasthani jali carving, Kutchi lippan mirror work, ajrakh block printing, Jodhpur blue pottery — but filters everything through a single, disciplined analogous palette. The result is an interior that feels culturally deep and visually unified.

Furniture & seating

Carved, inlaid, and painted in the river's own colours.

Sheesham carved chairs

Hand-carved sheesham chairs painted deep teal with distressed finish. Seats upholstered in indigo ikat fabric with gold piping. Back panels with simple jali (lattice) cutout. Source: Saharanpur wood carvers. Budget: ₹5,000–14,000/chair.

Mughal arch benches

Mughal arch-back wooden bench in carved wood, painted peacock teal. Seat upholstered in ajrakh block-print fabric. 150cm bench seats 3. Against walls, creates a strong architectural silhouette. Source: Rajasthan joinery workshops.

Inlaid tables

Sheesham tables with inlaid brass geometric border patterns (traditional Rajasthani brass inlay). Round 70cm for 2-tops. One central low table with Jodhpur blue pottery tile mosaic top — hand-set tiles in the analogous palette.

Jali screen dividers

Carved jali wooden screens as zone dividers — visual separators that allow light and air through. Painted deep indigo. They cast geometric shadow patterns when lit from one side — the room's most magical lighting effect.

Charpai-style bench

Low charpai-inspired bench seating with woven jute base on teak frame. Rolled cylindrical cushions in ajrakh fabric. Floor-level seating option beside window, creating a relaxed zone distinct from the dining areas.

Textiles guide

Seat fabric: ajrakh block-print cotton in blue resist dye (Kutch, Gujarat). Cushions: indigo hand-dyed with batik finish. Table runners: ikat-woven silk-cotton blend in teal. Curtains: deep ocean blue heavy cotton. Source: Kutch or Bagru craft cooperatives.

Indian craft vocabulary

Six crafts that define the space.

Ajrakh block print

Traditional resist-print from Kutch, Gujarat. Geometric interlocking patterns in indigo and teal on natural cotton. On seat cushions, table runners, and curtain panels. Deep cultural resonance — immediately recognisable as Indian craft excellence.

Jali lattice work

Perforated geometric screens from Rajasthani architecture. Used as room dividers, window inserts, ceiling light panels, and bar-back screens. When backlit: creates the signature geometric light-shadow play across the entire room. Can be laser-cut in MDF for affordability.

Jodhpur blue pottery

Hand-painted pottery in the analogous palette. Coffee cups (custom order with café logo), display tiles for countertop backsplash, small bowl sets, and wall-mounted display plates. Factory: Kripal Kumbh, Jaipur — they do custom orders with your design.

Lippan mirror art

Kutchi clay + mirror work. Commission a full 2×2m wall panel in teal and gold geometric patterns with embedded mirrors. Mirrors scatter light across the room. This is simultaneously art, texture, and the entire mirror strategy for this theme.

The Ganga mural

One full wall painted as an abstract river scene — deep indigo at the base rising through teal, peacock, mint to near-white at the top. Done in lime wash or mineral paint. Commission a local Rishikesh artist. This becomes the café's signature photograph backdrop.

Pierced brass lanterns

Rajasthani/Mughal-style pierced brass lanterns hanging at varying heights over the bar. Inside: warm amber bulbs. The pierced pattern casts intricate geometric shadows across walls and floor. Group 5–7 at different heights for a dramatic focal constellation.

"The Ganga holds every shade of blue. This cafe holds every shade of the Ganga."
Façade & floor plan

Entering through a carved gate into the river.

Exterior façade

Deep indigo blue lime-plaster exterior. Carved wooden jali panel inset into the entrance arch. Blue Rajasthani mosaic tiles as a border frame around the doorway. Hanging brass bells at entry. Hand-painted café name in Devanagari + English in gold paint on a deep blue board.

Counter & lighting

Hand-laid mosaic tile counter in blue-teal geometric pattern. Brass trim along counter edge. Deep indigo-painted wood panelling below counter. Primary lighting: pierced brass lanterns. Secondary: recessed warm white to illuminate the Ganga mural from below. Evening: lower main lights, only lanterns active — the room becomes a constellation.

Zone layout

Entrance wall: Full Ganga mural — this is the first thing customers see.

Left zone: Mughal arch banquette seating + inlaid brass tables.

Centre: Carved sheesham chairs + jali screen dividers.

Right zone: Charpai floor seating + low tables near windows.

Bar: Mosaic tile counter with brass lantern cluster above. Lippan mirror panel behind the bar — reflects the lantern constellation.

Schematic layout · not to scale · ~400 sqft
